Understanding Traditional Timber Joints

Timber joints are fundamental to joinery, providing strength and stability to assembled components. Each joint has a specific purpose.

Butt Joint
Description: The simplest joint, where one piece of timber is butted up against another and fixed with nails, screws, or adhesive. It is relatively weak.
Common Use: Joining wall frame plates to studs, or simple box constructions like basic kitchen cabinetry carcasses.

Mitre Joint
Description: A type of butt joint where the two pieces are cut at an angle, usually 45 degrees, to form a 90-degree corner. It provides a clean, seamless look.
Common Use: Picture frames, and decorative mouldings like window and door architraves.

Rebate (or Rabbet) Joint
Description: A recess or step cut along the edge of a piece of timber to receive another piece. It provides a better location for fixing than a simple butt joint.
Common Use: Setting back panels into carcasses or frames, such as the back of a bookshelf or cupboard.

Housing Joint (including Dado Joint)
Description: A slot or trench cut across the grain of a piece of timber to receive the end of another piece. A dado joint is a specific type of housing joint that is typically wider and used for different applications.
Common Use: Fixing shelves into the sides of a cupboard or bookshelf, stair construction (housing treads into stringers), shelving units, bookcase construction, and cabinet dividers where strong support is required.

Half-Lap Joint
Description: Formed by removing half the thickness of each piece of timber at the joint, so they overlap to form a flush surface.
Common Use: Simple frame constructions. A 'cross halving' joint is a variation used where two pieces cross over each other.

Lap Joint
Description: A variation of the half-lap joint where the overlap can be adjusted for different applications and strength requirements.
Common Use: Frame construction, box making, and anywhere a strong overlapping connection is needed.

Mortice and Tenon Joint
Description: A traditional, very strong joint. A square or rectangular hole (the mortice) is cut in one piece, and a corresponding tongue (the tenon) is cut on the end of the other piece to fit snugly inside.
Common Use: High-strength frame construction, such as for doors, window sashes, and furniture like tables and chairs.

Tongue and Groove Joint
Description: A joint made with a protruding tongue on the edge of one board and a corresponding groove on the edge of the next. It allows multiple boards to be joined together to form a wider panel.
Common Use: Solid timber flooring, wall panelling, and tabletop construction.

Biscuit Joint
Description: A modern joint where oval-shaped slots are cut into the edges of two pieces of timber. A compressed timber "biscuit" is covered in glue and inserted into the slots, expanding to create a strong, hidden joint.
Common Use: Joining boards edge-to-edge for tabletops or widening panels.

Dovetail Joint
Description: A strong interlocking joint where wedge-shaped projections (tails) on one piece fit into corresponding slots (pins) on the other piece. It resists pulling apart.
Common Use: High-quality drawer construction, box making, and fine furniture where strength and aesthetics are important.

Half-Blind Dovetail Joint
Description: A variation of the dovetail joint where the tails are not visible from one side, creating a cleaner appearance while maintaining strength.
Common Use: Drawer fronts and other applications where a clean, finished appearance is desired on one side.

Sliding Dovetail Joint
Description: A variation of the dovetail where one piece slides into a dovetail-shaped channel in another piece, creating a strong sliding connection.
Common Use: Drawer construction, sliding panels, and anywhere a strong sliding joint is needed.

Dowel Joint
Description: A joint where cylindrical wooden pins (dowels) are inserted into corresponding holes in both pieces to align and strengthen the connection.
Common Use: Frame construction, furniture assembly, and anywhere precise alignment and additional strength are needed.

Box Joint
Description: A series of interlocking rectangular fingers cut into the ends of two pieces, creating a strong corner connection.
Common Use: Box construction, drawer sides, and corner joints where strength and simple construction are priorities.

Bridle Joint
Description: A variation of the mortice and tenon where the mortice is open at the end, allowing the tenon to slide in from the side.
Common Use: Frame construction, particularly in timber framing and traditional joinery where the joint needs to be assembled after construction.

Pocket Joint
Description: A modern joint where angled holes are drilled into one piece to receive screws that connect it to another piece at an angle.
Common Use: Cabinet construction, face frame assembly, and anywhere hidden fasteners are desired for a clean appearance.
