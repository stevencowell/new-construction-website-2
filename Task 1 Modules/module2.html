The Risk Management Process

A systematic process is used on all construction sites to manage WHS risks. It involves four key steps:

  1. Identify Hazards: Proactively look for things in the workplace that have the potential to cause harm. This can be done through site walks, safety checklists, and talking to workers.
  2. Assess Risks: For each hazard identified, determine the level of risk. Consider how likely it is to happen and what the consequences would be (e.g., minor injury, serious injury, or fatality).
  3. Control Risks: Implement measures to eliminate the hazard or, if that's not possible, minimise the risk. This is done using the Hierarchy of Controls.
  4. Review Controls: Regularly check that the control measures are working as intended and haven't introduced new hazards.

The Hierarchy of Controls

This system ranks risk controls from the most effective to the least effective. You must always aim to implement controls as high up the hierarchy as practicable, combining measures where necessary. The order is:

  • Level 1: Eliminate the hazard
    • Physically remove the hazard altogether. Example: rather than working over a hole in the floor, fill the opening before commencing work.
  • Level 2: Substitute
    • Replace the hazard with something safer. Example: use a water-based paint instead of a solvent-based paint, or a lighter material that can be installed without mechanical lifting.
  • Level 3: Isolate
    • Separate people from the hazard. Example: install fencing or barriers around an excavation, or enclose a noisy machine in a sound-proof room.
  • Level 4: Engineering controls
    • Use mechanical devices or modify equipment to reduce exposure. Example: fit machine guards, ventilation systems or lifting equipment such as hoists and mechanical vacuum lifters.
  • Level 5: Administrative controls
    • Change the way work is organised. This includes implementing safe work procedures, providing training and supervision, scheduling tasks to reduce exposure time, and using signage to warn of hazards.
  • Level 6: Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
    • Provide workers with protective equipment such as hard hats, gloves, respirators and fall-arrest harnesses. PPE is the least effective control because it relies on human behaviour and does not remove the hazard; it should be used only when higher-level controls cannot fully eliminate or minimise the risk.
