Supplementary Reference Notes - Task 4: Working It Out

This module will guide you through the process of creating an estimate and then comparing it to the final, actual costs, as demonstrated in your "Carry All Tool Box" project.

Estimating vs. Actual Costs

  • Estimated Cost: This is your calculated prediction of what the project will cost, prepared before work begins. You base this on the plans and supplier price lists. This is the figure you would give to a client in a quote.
  • Actual Cost: This is the real amount of money spent on materials and labour after the project is finished.

The Costing Process

  1. Create a Material List (Your Take-Off): Look at the project plans (e.g., the "Carry All Tool Box" drawings) and list every single piece of material required, noting its dimensions.
  2. Estimate Material Costs:
    • Take your list and look at your supplier's price list (e.g., the "Plainfield Building Supplies" list).
    • For each item, find the most economical size to buy. For example, if you need a piece of pine that is 820mm long for the handle, you must buy the next available length, which might be 0.9m (900mm).
    • Calculate the cost for each item and add it all up for your estimated material total.
  3. Estimate Labour Costs:
    • Break the entire project down into a series of smaller tasks (e.g., "Measure and cut components," "Assemble parts," "Sand finish").
    • Estimate how many hours or minutes each task will realistically take.
    • Use the wage rate sheet (e.g., the "CFMEU Award") to find the correct hourly rate for the person doing the work (e.g., a first-year apprentice).
    • Calculate the labour cost for each task and add them up for your estimated labour total.
  4. Calculate the Total Estimated Cost: Add your material and labour estimates together.
    • Total Estimate = Estimated Material Cost + Estimated Labour Cost

Why does the estimate differ from the actual cost?

It's very common for the final cost to be different from the estimate. The assessment asks you to consider why this might happen. Think about:

  • Time: Did a task take longer than expected because of a mistake or a tricky step? This increases actual labour costs.
  • Material Wastage: Did you make a wrong cut and have to use a new piece of timber? This can increase material costs.
  • Price Changes: Supplier prices might change between the time of the estimate and the time of purchase.

Understanding these differences is key to becoming a better estimator. The assessment notes that inaccurate estimation can mean you either short-change yourself or you do not get the job because your quote is too expensive.
