Sequencing a Construction Procedure

Before you start building, you need a logical plan. Sequencing involves breaking the project down into steps and putting them in the correct order. For a basic wall frame, the sequence is critical.

  1. Review Plans: Always start by reading the plans and specifications to understand what you need to build.
  2. Gather Materials: Collect all the timber and other materials required.
  3. Measure and Mark: Carefully measure and mark all your components (plates, studs, noggins) to the correct lengths. A common rule is "measure twice, cut once" to minimise errors.
  4. Cut Components: Cut all pieces to their marked lengths. Double-check your measurements after cutting.
  5. Mark Out Joints & Layout: Lay the top and bottom plates together and mark the positions for all the studs. Mark out any rebates or housings that need to be cut.
  6. Assemble Frame: Lay out the components on a flat surface. Fit the studs into position on the plates and nail them together.
  7. Install Noggins: Measure the distance between the studs, cut the noggins to fit snugly, and nail them into place.
  8. Check for Square: Use a tape measure to check the diagonal measurements of the completed frame. If the two diagonal measurements are equal, the frame is square. If not, it needs to be adjusted before bracing.

Completing a Materials List

As covered in previous tasks, a materials list requires you to extract all necessary components from the project plans. For each component, you must list:

  • Component Name: e.g., Corner Studs, Top Plate.
  • Number Required (No. Req'd): The total count of that component.
  • Width, Thickness, Length: The individual dimensions of one piece.
  • Total Length: The total linear metres needed for that component type (e.g., 2 studs at 850mm each = 1700mm total length).
  • Material: The type of timber or material specified (e.g., Structural Pine).

Completing a Job Safety Analysis (JSA)

A JSA is a formal process for managing safety for a specific task.

  1. List Work Activities: Break the job down into the same steps you used for your construction procedure.
  2. Identify Potential Hazards: For each activity, think about what could go wrong and cause an injury (e.g., for "Cut timber," the hazards are cuts from the saw blade, entanglement, noise, and flying dust/debris).
  3. Assess the Risk (Pre-Risk): Use the risk matrix provided in your assessment to give the hazard a risk score *before* any controls are applied.
  4. List Hazard Controls: Write down the specific actions you will take to make the task safer (e.g., "Use saw guard," "Keep hands 150mm from blade," "Wear safety glasses and earmuffs").
  5. Re-assess the Risk (Post-Risk): Give the task a new risk score *after* your controls have been applied. This score should be lower than the pre-risk score.
  6. Assign Responsibility: Name the person responsible for ensuring the controls are followed (usually the person doing the work).
