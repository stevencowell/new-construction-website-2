Working Effectively in a Construction Team

Construction is a team sport. Your ability to work well with others is just as important as your practical skills.

  • Key Skills for Teamwork:
    • Communication Skills: Clearly sharing information, listening to instructions, and asking questions when you're unsure.
    • Problem-Solving Skills: Working together to find solutions when issues arise.
    • Planning and Sequencing: Collaborating with the team to plan the order of work.
    • General and Site-Specific WHS Knowledge: Understanding safety rules and looking out for your teammates.
  • Effective Team Techniques:
    • Work to the best of your ability and take pride in your contribution.
    • Encourage and assist others when they need help.
    • Don't be afraid to ask for assistance yourself when you need it.
    • Communicate clearly about your progress and any delays.

Construction Industry Careers

Your VET course is the first step. There are many career pathways in the construction industry.

  • Carpenter: A very large and growing trade. Carpenters usually work on-site, erecting frames, roofs, and installing fixtures.
  • Joiner: A more specialised trade that usually works in a workshop, manufacturing detailed timber components like windows, doors, and stairs.
  • Construction Manager: Oversees entire projects, from planning to completion. This role requires extensive experience and often further qualifications.
  • Building Estimator: A specialised role, often office-based, responsible for calculating the costs of materials, labour, and equipment to prepare project budgets and quotes.

Environmental & Resource Efficiency

The construction industry has a significant environmental impact. It is everyone's responsibility to minimise this.

  • Reducing Waste: This is a key principle.
    • Careful Ordering: Accurately calculate material needs to avoid ordering excess.
    • Careful Use: Maximise the use of materials to reduce offcuts and wastage.
    • Waste Separation: Separate waste on-site into different bins for recyclable materials (metal, cardboard), reusable materials (e.g., timber offcuts), and general waste.
  • Common Environmental Hazards:
    • Improper disposal of packaging.
    • Spills of hazardous chemicals (fuel, oil, paint).
    • Dust from cutting and moving materials.
    • Fire risk from on-site heat sources.
    • Incorrect handling of hazardous materials like asbestos or treated timber (e.g., CCA). **Note:** Burning CCA treated timber is illegal and dangerous as it releases toxic arsenic into the air.
